## LinkedIn

I've been watching the AI implementation conversations in business circles for a while now, and I keep seeing the same pattern play out.

A company gets excited about AI. They invest time, money, resources. They launch a pilot. And then... nothing.

The stats are grim. 95% of AI projects fail to deliver measurable value. 42% of companies have abandoned most AI initiatives in the past year.

After digging into what's actually going wrong, I think it comes down to five things:

**1. The data is a mess**

This one almost goes without saying, but it's still the number one issue. Garbage in, garbage out. You can't build good AI on top of scattered, inconsistent, incomplete data.

**2. Unrealistic expectations about time**

AI isn't magic. It's not a light switch. But companies often treat it like one—expecting transformative results in weeks when they should be thinking in quarters or years.

**3. No one inside actually knows how to use it**

I've seen this repeatedly. A company buys a tool but doesn't invest in building internal expertise. They expect the tool to just... work. It doesn't work that way.

**4. AI is designed for workflows, not for humans**

This is the one that keeps me up at night, and it's the root cause of so many failures.

We evaluate AI by how much it accelerates a process. How many tasks it can automate. How much time it saves.

But here's the thing—humans don't care about processes.

When I sit down to think about a problem, I don't care about workflows. I care about:

- Understanding the full context
- Making unexpected connections
- Following my own thinking as it naturally unfolds
- Getting answers that respect how I already work

Most AI systems I've encountered force me to adapt to them instead of the other way around.

"Use the right prompt."
"Structure your input this way."
"Break it down into these specific chunks."

That's not adaptation. That's training the human to serve the tool.

**5. We're measuring the wrong outcomes**

The ROI questions companies ask about AI are all wrong. They're about efficiency, speed, cost reduction.

But the real value of AI in knowledge work isn't about any of those things. It's about helping people think better, make better decisions, do work that matters.
